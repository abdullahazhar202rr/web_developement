Is Calculus hard?

Calculus is "the mathematics of change". Unfortunately, many people think that means calculus is hard.

But we know from experience that it's not true. Calculus isn't supposed to be hard; what's hard about calculus is the way it's taught! As teachers, we can help our students discover how rewarding and empowering calculus can be.

Calculus is not intrinsically difficult. It may be new, but it's also natural and logical, not to mention beautiful! Calculus is all about change - rates of change, areas under curves, volumes inboxes. What could be more familiar? How can we convey to students that calculus is just about change and that the subject itself isn't hard?

Well, for starters, let's not present calculus as a list of isolated facts. Many students have exactly this experience: they are shown something new called "the derivative", told what it is good for, given an example or two, and then they are tested on it. A few days later, they're shown something new called "the integral", told what it is good for, given an example or two, and again they are tested on it. 

But if calculus were really about rates of change - which of course it is - then it would be pure insanity to present these topics separately, as though they had nothing to do with each other.

That's not how we think about change in the "real world". We don't separate rates of change from areas under curves or volumes inboxes. We didn't evolve brains that think like that! We think about change holistically, as a seamless whole.

If we present calculus in this holistic way, then it will take longer for students to get through the material. As teachers, we can explain how worthwhile and enjoyable learning math in this natural way can be; but again, it may take a little more time.

Why is solving Calculus Assignment so difficult?

Calculus is a difficult subject, even for those who are good at math. Students who are appearing in the examinations need to practice enough so that they can solve problems easily. 

There are many books available that contain solved Calculus problems to help students. However, their solutions might prove harmful rather than beneficial. Otherwise, you might not be able to understand what your mistakes are and end up making them again even if you try to practice solving Calculus problems on your own. 

5 most common mistakes students make while solving Calculus Assignment questions are:

  • Confusion between function and its representation

Normally, a function is represented in the form of y = f(x) where x is the independent variable and y is dependent. For example, ƒ(x) = 5x – 3 is a function whereas {1, 2, -3} represents a set or domain of all real numbers except -3.

  • Confusing between left-hand limit and right-hand limit

A function can be represented in various ways like, for ƒ(x) = x 2, the graph of this function is not symmetric about the y-axis. To obtain the left-hand limit of ƒ at x = a, you should consider x = - a. Similarly, to get the right-hand limit of ƒ at x = a, you must consider x = a.

  • Confusion between open interval and half-open interval

In Calculus questions related to limits involving infinity, there are two types of intervals – Open Interval and Half-Open Interval. To get the left-hand limit of a function ƒ at x = c, you must consider - ∞ < x < c. Similarly, to get the right-hand limit of ƒ at x = c, you must consider x = c.

  • Forgetting to take the absolute value of sine or cosine inside integration

Integration is the process of finding an antiderivative. For example, ƒ(x) = sinx and F (x) = x - cos x are anti-derivatives of each other. It is necessary to take absolute value while taking the derivative of a trigonometric function like sine or cosine because sine and cosine are represented as (-cosx, sinx).

  • Confusion between left-hand limit and right-hand limit of sine or cosine function

Integration is the process of finding an antiderivative and integration and differentiation are inverse operations. The derivative of sinx is cos x and the derivative of cos x will be sin x. So, if you are finding the left-hand limit of sinx at x = c, you must consider -c < x < c. Similarly, to find the right-hand limit of sinx at x = c, you must take x = -c.
