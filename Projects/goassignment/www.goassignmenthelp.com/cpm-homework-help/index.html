College Preparatory Mathematics or CPM includes the study of algebra methodology, geometry, probability & statistics, and discrete mathematics. The university CPM course sequence focuses on teaching students to reason abstractly about mathematical concepts.   • In the first year, students learn number sense, geometry, functions, statistics, and
  • In the second year, they focus on probability and algebra. 

It is crucial to learn CPM lessons, as well as the concepts that you learn, which are carried over to all upper-level math courses.

What do we study in the CPM Precalculus course?

A CPM precalculus course is a mathematics course that is intended to prepare students for further study in calculus or analysis. These courses exist as part of the curriculum at the high school and university levels, with university-level precalculus courses being more common at international universities. 

Most CPM Precalculus courses include topics from:

  • algebra, 
  • geometry,
  • trigonometry, and
  • experimental and fundamental calculus.

The course is designed to prepare students for higher-level mathematics such as Calculus I and II. Typically precalculus courses introduce: 

  • linear equations and inequalities; 
  • polynomials and the resultant; 
  • simultaneous linear equations; 
  • quadratic equations and functions; 
  • exponential and logarithmic functions; 
  • trigonometry (angles, conversions, addition formulas, double-angle, and half-angle formulas); and
  • conic sections (parabolas, ellipses, circles).

Why do students find CPM Homework Help Integrated 1, Integrated 2, and Integrated 3 problems difficult?

Calculus students are often presented with integration problems that require them to find the area between curves. These types of problems fall into Core Connections Integrated 1, Core Connection Integrated 2, and Core Connection Integrated 3 categories. 

Students can struggle when solving these problems. Sometimes, it is due to incorrect approaches that some teachers use while teaching these problems in the classroom. 

Students often go wrong when they:

  1. Use their calculators and run into possible round-off errors. This can be avoided if all computations were done with function values.
  2. Use limiting values that do not include the area between curves, and when they cross or touch each other at one point.
  3. Compute directly from what was given in the problem.
